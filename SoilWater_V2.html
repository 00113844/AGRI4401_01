Part 1: Why Soil and Water Vary Together

System interactions and landscape frameworks

A Single, Jointly Variable System

Soil and water spatial variability are not separate phenomena—they are two faces of one system.

  • Textural Control: Sand drains quickly; clay retains moisture and can cause surface ponding.
  • Landscape Dynamics: Topography directs water, driving differential soil weathering and development over time.
  • The Two-Way Relationship: Historical water movement establishes today's patterns; current properties dictate future water behavior.
Open by establishing the central idea of the lecture: soil and water spatial variability are not two separate phenomena to study side by side, but two faces of one system. Where a soil is sandy, water drains quickly and deeply; where it is clay-rich, water is retained near the surface and may pond. Where a landscape position collects water, soil weathers and develops differently than where water sheds away. The properties developed across this lecture — texture, structure, pH, porosity — are largely the consequences of how water has moved across and through a landscape over very long timescales, and they in turn control how water moves through that landscape today. Preview the lecture's organising question: how does this two-way soil-water relationship create the spatial patterns precision agriculture must detect, map, and manage?

Part 2: How Soil Forms — Pedogenesis

The drivers of long-term landscape differentiation

The Five Factors of Soil Formation

  • Parent Material: Starting rock/sediment substrate.
  • Climate: Temperature and rainfall driving weathering rates.
  • Organisms: Biological inputs, roots, and organic breakdown.
  • Relief: Topography directing water and erosion/deposition.
  • Time: The absolute duration of process action.

Introduce the classical framework for understanding why soils differ from place to place: parent material (the rock or sediment a soil weathers from), climate (rainfall and temperature driving the rate and type of weathering), organisms (plant roots, soil fauna, and microbes contributing organic matter and structure), relief (the local topography controlling water movement and erosion/deposition, directly connecting to the toposequence concept introduced in the companion Nutrient Spatial Variability chapter), and time (the duration over which these processes have acted). Explain that every soil-forming process developed in the rest of this Part is, ultimately, one of these five factors acting on a landscape, and that spatial variability in soil properties arises precisely because these five factors are themselves spatially variable.

Weathering: Breaking Down Substrates

Physical Weathering

Mechanical fracture without compositional change (freeze-thaw, thermal action). Historically less prominent in Western Australia's mild, ice-free post-glacial eras.

Chemical Weathering

Mineral transformation via hydrolysis, oxidation, and dissolution. Highly moisture and temperature dependent.

The WA Context: Prolonged tectonic stability has allowed uninterrupted chemical weathering to shape deeply distinct landscapes.

Explain the two broad weathering mechanisms that begin the transformation of solid parent material into soil. Physical weathering breaks rock into smaller fragments without changing its chemical composition — through freeze-thaw cycling, thermal expansion and contraction, and abrasion, processes of limited relevance to WA's generally mild, ice-free climate. Chemical weathering alters the mineral composition itself — through hydrolysis, oxidation, dissolution, and hydration reactions — and is strongly temperature- and moisture-dependent, proceeding faster under warm, wet conditions. Explain why this distinction matters for WA specifically: the state's long, climatically stable, largely ice-free geological history has allowed chemical weathering to dominate and proceed largely uninterrupted for an extraordinarily long time, the central fact behind the deeply weathered soils profiled later in this Part.

Horizon Development

  • A Horizon (Topsoil): Enriched with accumulating organic matter; dark coloration.
  • B Horizon (Subsoil): Target zone for leached materials (clays, iron, aluminum compounds). Highly critical for phosphorus-fixing behavior.
  • C Horizon (Regolith): Weathered parent material sitting beneath the active soil profile.

Spatial variability in horizon thickness and boundary sharpness is dictated by topography-driven water distribution.

Explain how a weathered parent material develops into a structured soil profile through organic matter accumulation and horizon differentiation. Organic matter accumulates at the surface from plant litter, root turnover, and microbial biomass, darkening and enriching the uppermost layer. Over time, distinct horizons develop: the A horizon (topsoil, enriched in organic matter), the B horizon (subsoil, where clay, iron, and aluminium compounds leached from above accumulate — directly relevant to the phosphorus-fixing behaviour of WA's iron/aluminium-oxide-rich subsoils introduced in the companion Nutrient Spatial Variability chapter), and the C horizon (weathered parent material, not yet fully transformed into soil). The depth, thickness, and clarity of these horizons differ enormously across a landscape depending on the other four formation factors, producing much of the physical and chemical spatial variability developed in Parts 3–4.

WA's Ancient Landscape Architecture

The Yilgarn Craton forms a highly distinct, structurally stable canvas:

  • Extraordinary Age: Hundreds of millions of years without rejuvenating glaciations or major mountain-building events.
  • Extreme Leaching: Deeply weathered profiles characterized by extensive lateritic gravels and severe nutrient depletion.
  • Agronomic Imperative: Inherent low fertility demands highly precise management of phosphorus and critical micronutrients.
Ground the weathering and horizon-development concepts in WA's distinctive geological story. Much of the WA wheatbelt sits on the Yilgarn Craton, among the oldest and most tectonically stable landmasses on Earth, with no glaciation and minimal mountain-building to physically rejuvenate the surface for hundreds of millions of years. The practical consequence: chemical weathering has had an unusually long, uninterrupted time to act, producing some of the most deeply weathered, leached soil profiles found anywhere in the world — the deep lateritic gravel and duplex profiles introduced as WA soil classes in the companion Nutrient Spatial Variability chapter are a direct expression of this geological age, not an incidental local quirk. This explains why WA's inherent soil fertility is generally low compared to younger, less-weathered agricultural landscapes elsewhere in the world, and why phosphorus and trace element management have historically been such central concerns of WA agronomy.

Part 3: Soil Physical Properties & Spatial Patterns

Texture, structure, bulk density, and topo-sequences

Soil Texture: The Baseline Matrix

  • Sand (2.0 – 0.05 mm): High macroporosity, rapid drainage, minimal retention.
  • Silt (0.05 – 0.002 mm): Intermediate pore structure.
  • Clay (< 0.002 mm): Massive specific surface area, high nutrient/water retention.

Texture remains effectively constant across human management timescales, defining the permanent physical template of the landscape.

graph TD T[Soil Texture] --> Sand[Sand: Coarse] T --> Silt[Silt: Intermediate] T --> Clay[Clay: Fine] style T fill:#27348B,color:#fff
Define soil texture as the relative proportion of three particle size classes — sand (coarse, 2–0.05 mm), silt (intermediate, 0.05–0.002 mm), and clay (fine, below 0.002 mm) — and explain why texture is one of the most fundamental properties governing almost everything else covered in this lecture. Coarser, sandier textures have larger particles and larger pore spaces between them, draining quickly and holding little water; finer, clay-rich textures have enormous internal surface area per unit volume, retaining water and nutrients far more strongly. Texture is also, for practical purposes, a relatively fixed property at human management timescales — unlike structure (the next slide), which management actively influences — making it the most stable, predictable layer of spatial variability within a paddock, and the property most directly inherited from the parent material and weathering history developed in Part 2.

Soil Structure: Aggregates and Stability

  • Definition: The spatial organization of primary particles into cohesive structural units ("peds").
  • Stabilizing Agents: Driven heavily by organic matter glues, microbial activity, and clay mineral properties.
  • The Sodicity Risk: High exchangeable sodium fraction ($Na^+$) causes hydration-driven dispersion, collapsing pores and sealing surfaces.
  • Dynamic Management: Highly sensitive to tillage, mechanical compaction, and biological residue preservation.
Distinguish soil structure from texture: structure describes how individual sand, silt, and clay particles bind together into larger aggregates ("peds"), creating the pore network through which air, water, and roots actually move — developed fully in Part 5. Structural stability depends on organic matter (acting as a binding agent between particles), clay mineralogy, and — critically for WA soils — sodicity: a high proportion of exchangeable sodium on clay exchange sites causes clay particles to disperse rather than aggregate when wetted, collapsing structure and sealing the soil surface, a problem already introduced in the context of subsoil constraints in the companion Nutrient Spatial Variability chapter. Unlike texture, structure is actively influenced by management: tillage, traffic, and organic matter inputs can degrade or rebuild it within a single farmer's working lifetime, making it the property most directly responsive to the management decisions covered later in this lecture.

Porosity and Bulk Density

Porosity ($\Phi$) and Bulk Density ($\rho_b$) share an inverse mathematical and physical relationship.

Well-Structured Matrix

Abundant organic inputs, high macroporosity, low bulk density. Ideal for root respiration and clean drainage lines.

Compacted Matrix

Mechanical load collapse, loss of structural voids, elevated bulk density. Severe physical impedance to roots.

Define porosity as the proportion of total soil volume occupied by pore space rather than solid particles, and bulk density as the mass of dry soil per unit volume — two properties that move inversely with each other and that directly reflect the combined outcome of texture and structure. A well-structured soil with abundant organic matter has high porosity and low bulk density; a compacted or naturally dense soil has low porosity and high bulk density. These two measurements are the standard, directly quantifiable way of characterising how much room a soil actually has for the water, air, and root growth developed in the rest of this lecture — and, as developed fully in Part 7, the standard way of quantifying compaction damage and verifying the soil-structure benefit claimed for Controlled Traffic Farming.

The Integrated Baseline

  • All hydrodynamic behaviors—from infiltration speed to perched water tables—emerge directly from the texture-structure template.
  • Physical layouts dictate chemical parameters: clay content controls total surface charge density and localized buffering mechanics.
  • Accurate agronomic modeling views the soil profile as an integrated, co-dependent unit.

Part 4: Soil Chemical Properties

pH dynamics, CEC, and system integration

Soil pH and Spatial Acidification

  • The Drivers: Ammonium-based fertilizers, organic matter breakdown, and deep leaching of alkaline basic cations.
  • Buffering Capacity: Sand profiles exhibit low buffering and acidify quickly; clay matrices resist pH flux due to high baseline exchange capacity.
  • The Precision Ag Challenge: Standard composite paddock testing obscures severe localized subsoil acid bands, hiding aluminum toxicity risks.
Introduce soil pH as a chemical property with its own distinct spatial pattern, complementing the cation exchange capacity content already developed in the companion Nutrient Spatial Variability chapter. pH governs the chemical form and availability of most nutrients and the solubility of aluminium (the toxicity mechanism addressed there using gypsum and lime). Explain why pH varies spatially within a single WA paddock: it reflects the cumulative balance of acidifying processes — nitrogen fertiliser use, organic matter decomposition, and leaching of basic cations on more free-draining soils — against buffering capacity, itself driven by clay content and type (Part 3) and organic matter. Sandy, low-buffering soils (the gravel rise in the running example) acidify faster and more severely for the same management history than a clay-rich, well-buffered soil (the valley floor), meaning a single paddock-average pH test can mask a genuinely large within-paddock range — directly paralleling the composite-sampling limitation introduced for nutrients in the companion chapter.

CEC and Textural Interdependence

Cation Exchange Capacity (CEC) is fundamentally a chemical consequence of physical layout conditions.

  • Clay fractions and organic complexes simultaneously define structural stability and electrostatic ion retention capacity.
  • Unified Soil Classes allow reliable concurrent prediction of physical fluid dynamics and chemical nutrient fixing potentials.
Briefly revisit cation exchange capacity and nutrient-holding capacity, developed in full in the companion Nutrient Spatial Variability chapter, specifically to close the conceptual loop with the physical properties just covered. CEC is, in essence, a chemical consequence of physical properties: clay content and mineralogy, and organic matter content, are simultaneously the primary drivers of both CEC (Part 4 of that companion chapter) and structural stability (Part 3 of this lecture). This is why a soil class description — such as the WA soil classes profiled in that companion chapter — can predict both physical behaviour (drainage, structure) and chemical behaviour (nutrient retention, fixation) from the same underlying texture and mineralogy information, rather than requiring two entirely separate descriptions.

Part 5: Soil Water Physics

Pore architecture, hydrodynamic thresholds, and duplex systems

Pore Architecture: Macro vs Micro

  • Macropores: Large gaps between structural aggregates, root lines, and faunal tracks. Evacuate quickly under gravitational pull; vital for gas exchange.
  • Micropores: Small internal matrix gaps. Retain fluids via strong capillary and absorptive forces against gravity.
graph TD P[Total Voids] --> Macro[Macropores: Rapid Drainage / Air] P --> Micro[Micropores: Capillary Fluid Retention] style P fill:#27348B,color:#fff
Introduce the pore-size distinction that governs how water actually behaves within the porosity described in Part 3. Macropores — the larger pores between aggregates, along root channels, and created by soil fauna — allow water to drain rapidly under gravity and provide the air-filled pathways roots and soil organisms depend on for respiration; they empty quickly after rain and are the first structural feature lost under compaction (developed fully in Part 7). Micropores — the smaller pores within and between fine particles, dominant in clay-rich material — retain water against gravity through capillary and adsorptive forces, holding the water that remains available to plant roots after macropores have drained. This single distinction is the physical basis for almost every concept in the rest of this Part: infiltration rate, field capacity, and available water capacity are all, fundamentally, statements about the relative proportion and connectivity of macropores versus micropores in a given soil.

Infiltration Mechanics

  • Initial Stage: High intake rates driven by gravity flow through open macropores.
  • Steady-State Stage: Rates level off as macro-voids fill, bounded by matrix capillary conductivity.
  • Surface Sealing Restraints: Impact from raindrops breaks down weak surface structures, triggering structural seal sheets. This increases runoff and accentuates water erosion risks.
Explain infiltration as the rate at which water enters the soil surface and moves downward, governed jointly by surface structural condition, texture, and the soil's existing moisture state. A dry, well-structured soil with abundant macropores infiltrates rapidly at first, since gravity-driven flow through large, connected pores dominates; as the profile wets up and macropores fill, the infiltration rate slows toward a steadier rate governed more by the finer pore matrix. Surface sealing — structural collapse at the very surface, often from raindrop impact on a poorly aggregated or sodic soil (Part 3) — can dramatically reduce infiltration regardless of the bulk soil's texture, increasing runoff and erosion risk, a direct physical mechanism behind the water-erosion spatial patterns introduced in the companion Crop Spatial Variability chapter.

Reference Hydro-States

  1. Saturation: Every void filled; gravity drainage active.
  2. Field Capacity: Gravity flow ceases; fluid is stabilized inside micropores.
  3. Permanent Wilting Point: Tension forces exceed plant root extraction capabilities.
graph TD S[1. Saturation: All Voids Full] --> FC[2. Field Capacity: Macro Voids Drained] FC --> PWP[3. Permanent Wilting Point: Extraction Limit] style FC fill:rgba(39,52,139,0.1),stroke:#27348B
Define the three reference water states that anchor every practical soil-water and irrigation calculation. Saturation: every pore, macro and micro, filled with water — the wettest possible state, typically short-lived as macropores drain rapidly under gravity. Field capacity: the water content remaining once gravity drainage from macropores has effectively stopped, with water held in micropores against gravity by capillary and adsorptive forces — the practical upper limit of water a soil can hold and make available to a crop. Permanent wilting point: the water content below which a plant can no longer extract water against the capillary forces holding it in the smallest micropores, at which point the plant wilts irrecoverably. Explain that these are not sharp physical thresholds but practical reference points along a continuous soil-water-retention relationship that differs for every soil texture and structure.

Available Water Capacity (AWC)

$$AWC = \theta_{FieldCapacity} - \theta_{WiltingPoint}$$

  • Gravel Rise: Low micropore volume results in narrow AWC bands and high drought risk.
  • Duplex Mid-Slope: Substantial subsoil potential, assuming roots can cross structural boundaries.
  • Valley Floor: High theoretical capacity, but frequently restricted by sodic layout conditions.
Define Available Water Capacity (AWC) as the difference between field capacity and permanent wilting point — the band of soil water content that is genuinely accessible to a crop, directly determining how long a crop can sustain growth between rainfall or irrigation events. Explain why AWC varies so dramatically across the running example paddock's three zones even though all three may receive identical rainfall: the gravel rise, with abundant macropores and little micropore capacity, has a narrow AWC and runs short of available water quickly in a dry spell; the duplex mid-slope's clay subsoil layer has a much larger AWC where roots can actually access it, but only if roots successfully penetrate the textural boundary; the valley floor's fine texture gives high theoretical AWC, but sodicity-restricted structure and a shallow saline water table can prevent roots from exploiting most of it. AWC is the single number that converts the soil-water physics of this Part into the practical question of how much drought risk a given zone of a paddock actually carries.

Texture & Structure Interaction

Identical textural classes yield completely divergent hydro-behaviors if structural integrity differs:

  • Compacted, degraded clay-loams behave like dense, fine sheets with reduced macroporosity.
  • Well-aggregated versions of the same texture maintain highly efficient drainage channels.
  • Structural Management (residue retention, controlled traffic) is the primary actionable lever available to farmers.
Reinforce that infiltration rate, field capacity, wilting point, and AWC are not independent soil properties but joint outcomes of texture and structure interacting together. Two soils with identical texture can have very different water behaviour if their structural condition differs — a compacted, structurally degraded clay loam can infiltrate and drain more like a poorly-aggregated fine-textured soil with reduced macroporosity, while a well-structured version of the same texture behaves quite differently. This is the direct, practical reason structural management (organic matter retention, traffic management, avoiding cultivation when wet) matters as much for water management as for the more commonly discussed compaction and yield concerns developed in Part 7 — structure is the lever a farmer can actually pull, while texture, set by formation history, is not.

Perched Water Tables: Duplex Soil Dynamics

  • Permeable sandy topsoil overlies a highly restrictive, low-permeability clay subsoil interface.
  • Infiltrating rain runs through topsoil quickly but ponds above the clay layer faster than it can clear.
  • Creates transient root zone waterlogging, suppressing gas exchange in key nutrition bands.
graph TD Rain[Rain Input] --> Sandy[Sandy Loam: Fast Flow] Sandy --> Interface((Clay Boundary)) Interface --> PWT[Perched Water Table Forms] Interface --> Slow[Clay Subsoil: Restricted Drainage] style PWT fill:#5577BB,color:#fff
Bring the running example's duplex mid-slope zone into full focus as a distinctive and widespread WA soil-water phenomenon. Where a permeable sandy or loamy topsoil sits over a much less permeable clay subsoil — the textural discontinuity introduced in Part 3 — infiltrating water can move through the topsoil quickly but then accumulate above the clay interface faster than it can drain through it, creating a temporary, "perched" water table within the profile, even on a gently sloping or well-drained-looking surface. This produces transient waterlogging that can persist for days to weeks after significant rainfall, restricting root oxygen supply exactly within the otherwise nutrient- and water-rich subsoil layer that roots most need to access — directly explaining the season-dependent instability of duplex-soil yield zones already introduced in the companion Crop Spatial Variability and Nutrient Spatial Variability chapters, here finally given its full physical mechanism.

Part 6: Root Zone Dynamics & Nutrient-Water Transport

Physical boundaries, leaching mechanics, and yield potential

Root Zone Restrictions

Theoretical water availability on paper is only realized if roots can physically reach it. Real-world boundaries include:

  • Physical Barriers: Severe compaction bands and elevated bulk density layers.
  • Chemical Barriers: Subsoil acidity ($Al^{3+}$ toxicity) and sodicity-induced dispersion.
  • Hydrological Barriers: Anoxic conditions from perched water tables or shallow, saline groundwater tables.
Define the root growth zone as the volume of soil a crop's root system can effectively occupy and extract water and nutrients from, and survey the constraints that can restrict it short of the soil's theoretical full depth. Physical constraints: compaction and high bulk density (Part 7) physically impede root penetration. Chemical constraints: subsoil acidity and aluminium toxicity, and sodicity-driven structural collapse, both introduced in the companion Nutrient Spatial Variability chapter, can make an otherwise accessible layer effectively unusable to roots. Water-table constraints: a perched water table (Part 5) or a permanent shallow saline water table (the running example's valley floor, developed fully in Part 9) can waterlog and de-oxygenate the root zone from below. Emphasise that an "available water capacity" calculated on paper (Part 5) is only realised in practice if roots can actually reach the layer holding it.

Nitrate-Water Leaching Mechanics

Nitrate ($NO_3^-$) is highly mobile and hitches a ride with bulk water movement:

  • On Free-Draining Rises: Heavy rainfall events flush nitrates through open macropores, driving them deep past root capture lines within days.
  • On Duplex Mid-Slopes: Perched water tables hold and pool nitrate concentrations above the clay boundary. This leads to lateral drainage export or sets up anaerobic denitrification losses.
Revisit nitrate mobility, introduced in the companion Nutrient Spatial Variability chapter, specifically through the water-movement lens developed in Part 5 of this lecture. Because nitrate moves essentially with the bulk flow of soil water rather than being held on exchange sites, its fate is directly governed by the infiltration and macropore/micropore behaviour just covered: on the free-draining gravel rise, nitrate applied ahead of a large rainfall event can leach through macropores well below the root zone within days; on the duplex mid-slope, a perched water table can temporarily concentrate nitrate within the saturated zone above the clay interface, then either move it laterally downslope once drainage resumes or expose it to the denitrification loss pathway already introduced in the companion chapter. This is a concrete illustration of how the same soil-water physics developed for irrigation and drought risk in this lecture directly governs nutrient fate as well.

Quantifying Yield Potential

$$\text{Total Seasonal Plant Available Water} \approx \text{Effective Rooting Depth} \times \text{AWC per Unit Depth}$$

  • Shallow root zones with high AWC often yield less than deep root zones with moderate baseline AWC.
  • Subsoil constraints create yield penalties that look wildly disproportionate relative to standard topsoil nutrient analysis checks.
Synthesise the root zone and available water capacity concepts into their combined effect on yield potential, particularly under WA's Mediterranean rainfall pattern and terminal drought risk, introduced in the companion Crop Spatial Variability chapter. Total plant-available water over a season is, to a first approximation, the product of effective rooting depth and available water capacity per unit depth — meaning a soil with excellent AWC per centimetre but a root-zone restriction at 30 cm depth can carry less total available water than a soil with modest AWC per centimetre but unrestricted roots to 100 cm. This is precisely why a subsoil constraint (sodicity, acidity, or a perched water table) can produce a yield penalty that looks, from the soil surface, disproportionate to anything visible in a standard topsoil test — the constraint is not reducing water availability in the textbook sense, it is reducing the volume of soil from which that water can actually be drawn.

Part 7: Compaction & Controlled Traffic Farming

Structural collapse mechanics and monitoring recovery

Compaction Damage Mechanics

  • Heavy field machinery traffic applies severe mechanical vertical loads.
  • Targeted Destruction: Large structural macropores collapse first, while small micropores remain largely unchanged.
  • Downstream Hydrological Impacts: Drop in surface infiltration rates (accelerating runoff), limited oxygen diffusion, and worse waterlogging on duplex clay plains.
Examine the soil-structural mechanism of compaction in detail, complementing the GNSS-and-guidance-focused treatment of Controlled Traffic Farming introduced in the companion GNSS and DGPS chapters. Machinery traffic applies mechanical load that collapses the larger structural pores first — macropores, formed by aggregation and biological activity, are far more vulnerable to collapse under load than the smaller micropores within individual particles — meaning compaction's first and most damaging effect is a disproportionate loss of macroporosity rather than a uniform reduction across all pore sizes. The practical consequences follow directly from Part 5's water physics: reduced macroporosity means reduced infiltration (more runoff and erosion risk), reduced aeration (root and microbial respiration impaired), and increased waterlogging risk in already waterlogging-prone duplex and clay soils — directly compounding, rather than merely adding to, the perched-water-table problem developed earlier in this lecture.

CTF: The Soil-Structural Evidence

Restricting all heavy wheel passes to permanent, repeatable pathways yields clear benefits:

  • Confines macropore collapse to dedicated sacrifice tracks, protecting the remaining crop beds.
  • WA Trial Evidence: Confirmed reductions in bulk density alongside significant increases in root-zone infiltration rates within a few seasons.
  • Biological Amplification: Undisturbed profiles allow root systems and soil fauna to build resilient macrochannels over time.
Connect to the GNSS-based CTF treatment in the companion GNSS and DGPS chapters by focusing specifically on the soil-structural evidence for its benefit. Confining all machinery traffic to permanent wheel tracks confines macropore collapse to those tracks alone, leaving the majority of the paddock area structurally intact across multiple seasons. Published WA trial evidence shows measurable bulk density reductions and infiltration rate improvements in the untrafficked zone within a few seasons of CTF adoption, with the improvement compounding as biological structure-building processes — root channels, earthworm and soil fauna activity, organic matter accumulation — are allowed to proceed undisturbed year after year in the same protected zone, rather than being reset by a randomly positioned pass every season under conventional traffic.

Verifying Profile Recovery

Structural recovery is a multi-season biological process, not an immediate fix.

  • Cone Penetrometers: Map physical mechanical resistance profiles across depth boundaries.
  • Core Sampling: Quantifies exact shifts in bulk density over time.
  • Farmers must plan for gradual, multi-year recovery paths as biology slowly re-establishes collapsed macro-void networks.
Address a practical and often underappreciated point: structural recovery from existing compaction is not instantaneous once traffic is confined, and verifying it requires direct measurement rather than assumption. Bulk density sampling and cone penetrometer profiling — introduced as mechanical sensing tools in the companion Sensors chapter — are the standard direct methods for confirming whether a compacted zone is actually recovering, and at what rate, once CTF or other remediation is adopted. Explain why recovery is typically gradual rather than immediate: rebuilding macroporosity depends on biological processes (root growth, faunal activity) and wetting-drying and freeze-thaw-style structural cycling, none of which occur instantly — meaning a farmer adopting CTF should expect and plan for a multi-season recovery trajectory, monitored with these tools, rather than a single-season fix.

Part 8: Measuring Spatial Variability — ECa & Mapping

Sensor physics, proxies, and digital soil modeling workflows

ECa Sensor Physics

1. Electromagnetic Induction

Transmitter coils induce internal electrical eddy currents in the soil; a receiver measures the secondary magnetic field response (e.g., EM38). Non-contact method.

2. Galvanic Contact

Coulter electrodes cut into the ground, injecting a known current and reading the voltage drop across inner discs (e.g., Veris). Requires direct contact.

Provide the measurement-physics explanation behind the apparent electrical conductivity (ECa) sensing already introduced as a zone-delineation tool in the companion Nutrient Spatial Variability chapter. Electromagnetic induction instruments (such as the EM38) use a transmitter coil to induce small electrical eddy currents within the soil itself, then measure the secondary magnetic field those currents produce with a receiver coil — a fully non-contact method that can be towed without touching the ground. Galvanic contact instruments (such as Veris systems) use electrodes dragged directly through the soil, passing a known current between outer electrodes and measuring the resulting voltage at inner electrodes — the same underlying four-electrode principle used in geophysical resistivity surveys — requiring physical soil contact but often giving a more controllable measurement depth. Both methods ultimately measure the same underlying soil property; they differ only in how the electrical signal is introduced and read.

Interpreting ECa Signals

ECa is a confounded proxy—it responds strongly to three distinct parameters simultaneously:

  • Clay Content/Type: High surface charge density leads to stronger electrical conductivity.
  • Moisture State: Water provides the necessary medium for ion transport across the soil matrix.
  • Salinity Level: Dissolved salts act directly as free charge carriers.

Caution: A high ECa signal alone cannot differentiate between a heavy clay patch, a fresh water-saturated zone, or a high-salinity hazard area without ground-truthing.

Consolidate, from the measurement-physics angle just established, why ECa correlates with so many fertility-relevant properties without directly measuring any of them. Clay content and mineralogy increase bulk conductivity because clay particles and their associated exchangeable ions conduct current more readily than sand; soil water content increases conductivity because water itself is the medium enabling ion movement; salinity increases conductivity directly, since dissolved salts are, by definition, charge carriers. Because all three properties move conductivity in the same direction, a single ECa reading cannot, by itself, distinguish a clay-rich zone from a wet zone from a saline zone — precisely the confounded-proxy caution already raised for EC in the companion Nutrient Spatial Variability chapter, here explained as an unavoidable consequence of the underlying physics rather than a sensor limitation that better engineering could remove.

Traditional Soil Survey Methods

  • Approach: Soil experts walk landscapes, dig inspection pits, use hand augers, and trace boundaries based on landscape markers.
  • Strengths: Delivers invaluable, direct field verification of horizon layouts, color changes, and structural properties.
  • Limitations: Highly labor-intensive, slow, and bound by coarse, discrete polygon boundaries rather than showing true continuous change.
Describe the conventional soil survey methodology that long preceded, and still underlies, digital soil mapping. A soil surveyor walks or augers a landscape at a planned sampling density, physically examining and describing soil profiles — horizon depths, texture, structure, colour, and other field-identifiable characteristics from Parts 2–3 of this lecture — and uses professional judgement, aided by landform and aerial photograph interpretation, to delineate boundaries between distinct soil mapping units on a paper or digital map. Explain the enduring value of this approach: it draws on direct, ground-truthed observation and pedological expertise that no sensor can fully replace, but it is slow, labour-intensive, and ultimately limited by surveyor density and the survey scale chosen — constraints that motivate the sensor-and-covariate-based digital approach developed in the next slide.

Digital Soil Mapping (DSM)

DSM replaces rigid map lines with continuous statistical property layers:

Spatial Covariates

High-resolution target inputs: RTK elevation models, ECa swaths, and multi-spectral satellite overviews.

Predictive Models

Machine learning algorithms link spatial covariates to a limited set of lab-analyzed physical samples.

Key Value: The system models the underlying relationship between landscape shapes and soil parameters, predicting properties across un-sampled spaces.

Build on the digital soil mapping concept already introduced from the spectroscopy angle in the companion Sensors chapter, here presenting the general modelling paradigm that spectroscopy is only one input to. Modern digital soil mapping combines multiple spatial covariate layers — terrain attributes derived from elevation data, ECa survey (this Part), spectral imagery (the companion Sensors chapter), and legacy traditional survey data (previous slide) — using a statistical or machine-learning model trained against a limited number of physically sampled and laboratory-analysed points, to predict a soil property continuously across an entire landscape. The key insight distinguishing this from simple interpolation: the model learns the relationship between covariates and the sampled property, then applies that relationship everywhere the covariates are known — including locations that were never physically sampled at all.

Skipping the independent validation stage is the most common and costly mistake in professional mapping pipelines.

Walk through the practical sequence of steps used to build a basic digital soil map for a paddock, synthesising the previous four slides into an actionable workflow. Step one: assemble available covariate layers — an ECa survey, an elevation model, and any existing imagery or legacy survey information. Step two: design a sampling scheme, typically guided by the covariate layers themselves (the zone-sampling logic introduced in the companion Nutrient Spatial Variability chapter), to capture the apparent range of variability efficiently. Step three: collect and laboratory-analyse the targeted samples. Step four: fit a predictive model relating the covariates to the sampled property values. Step five: validate the model against held-back samples not used in fitting, and only then generate the final continuous map. Emphasise that skipping the validation step — and simply trusting whatever map a model produces — is the most common and most consequential shortcut taken in practice.

Part 9: Groundwater, Drainage & Dryland Salinity

Aquifer mechanics, computational models, and remediation engineering

Groundwater Hydrology Foundations

  • Aquifer Matrix: Subsurface geological structures capable of storing and moving water.
  • Unconfined Aquifer: Upper water table is directly exposed to vertical rainfall recharge.
  • Confined Aquifer: Sealed under low-permeability clay or rock cap sheets.
  • The Hydro-Balance: Shifts in water table depth track the net balance between local recharge zones and down-valley discharge lines.
Introduce the basic vocabulary of groundwater hydrology needed to understand the rest of this Part. An aquifer is a geological formation capable of storing and transmitting groundwater; an unconfined aquifer has a water table open to recharge from directly above, while a confined aquifer is overlain by a low-permeability layer restricting direct vertical recharge. The water table is the upper surface of the saturated zone in an unconfined aquifer, varying in depth across a landscape and rising or falling with the balance between recharge (water entering, typically from rainfall infiltration beyond the root zone) and discharge (water leaving, whether to a river, the coast, or — critically for WA dryland salinity, developed later in this Part — directly to the surface at a valley floor or break-of-slope position). This recharge-discharge balance is the hydrological backbone of every groundwater application covered in the rest of this Part.

Simulating Flow Dynamics: MODFLOW

  • Overview: The international gold standard modular 3D finite-difference groundwater modeling framework.
  • Mechanics: Dissects subsurface profiles into structured grid cells, assigns specific hydraulic conductivity parameters, and calculates flux outputs.
  • Agronomic Application: Simulates how sweeping vegetation shifts (e.g., clearing deep perennials) impact regional water table heights over decades.
Introduce MODFLOW, the widely used modular groundwater flow simulation framework, as the standard computational tool for predicting how groundwater moves through an aquifer system over time. The model represents the subsurface as a three-dimensional grid of cells, each assigned hydraulic properties (such as permeability and storage capacity), and solves for groundwater flow between cells based on these properties, recharge and discharge inputs, and boundary conditions such as rivers, drains, or pumping bores. Explain its practical agricultural relevance: a groundwater flow model can predict how a change in land use or vegetation — such as the broadscale clearing of deep-rooted perennial vegetation central to the dryland salinity story developed two slides ahead — will alter the water table over years to decades, informing both the diagnosis of an existing salinity problem and the design of a drainage or revegetation response intended to correct it.

Groundwater Quality & Pollution Risk

Nutrient actions in topsoil zones eventually turn into downstream groundwater quality issues.

  • Leached nitrates passing below sandy root zones migrate along regional aquifer flow paths.
  • Accumulating nitrate pools threaten rural livestock water options and fragile off-site river systems.
  • Mobilized salt stores transform rising deep water tables into direct threats to surface crop safety.
Extend the groundwater discussion to water quality, connecting directly to the nitrate leaching content developed in Part 6. Nitrate that leaches below the root zone on free-draining soils, such as the running example's gravel rise, does not simply disappear — it continues moving with groundwater flow and can accumulate in an aquifer over years of repeated leaching events, with implications for both downstream ecosystem health and the suitability of that groundwater for stock or domestic use. Mobilised salinity, developed fully in the next two slides, is itself a groundwater quality issue as much as a soil issue. Briefly note other agricultural groundwater quality risks — pesticide residues and effluent from intensive livestock operations — as further examples of the same general principle: anything that leaches below the root zone (Part 6) becomes, eventually, a groundwater question rather than a soil question.

The Dryland Salinity Chain

1. Native Ecosystem

Deep-rooted, perennial vegetation transpires water year-round, keeping water tables deep.

2. Broad Clearing

Shallow annual crops leave fields dormant for months, increasing deep drainage recharge.

3. Saline Outbreak

Water tables lift, dissolving ancient regolith salts and discharging into low valley floors.

Explain the well-documented mechanistic chain behind WA's dryland salinity problem, building directly on the groundwater concepts just introduced. Native WA vegetation, deeply rooted and active year-round, historically used most of the rainfall reaching a landscape through transpiration, keeping recharge to groundwater low and water tables deep. Broadscale clearing for annual cropping replaced this vegetation with shallower-rooted, seasonally dormant crops and pastures that use substantially less water overall, increasing recharge and causing water tables to rise across much of the cleared wheatbelt over subsequent decades. As water tables rise, they mobilise salt that has accumulated in the deeply weathered WA regolith over geological time (Part 2) and bring it toward the surface, discharging at valley floors and break-of-slope positions — precisely the running example's saline valley-floor zone — where it damages or kills salt-intolerant crops and pasture.

Engineering and Hydrological Interventions

  • Deep Open Drains: Physically intercept and route saline groundwater out of valley floors, requiring significant capital investment.
  • Raised-Bed Infrastructure: Elevates active crop root zones safely above perched water bands in high-rainfall regions.
  • Perennial Plant Phases: Alternating phases of lucerne, perennial grasses, or saltbush help restore year-round transpiration, lowering water tables naturally.
Survey the practical management responses available once a dryland salinity or waterlogging problem, of the kind developed in this Part and in Part 5's perched-water-table discussion, has been diagnosed. Deep open or sub-surface tile drains intercept and remove saline or perched groundwater before it reaches the surface or root zone, at meaningful capital and ongoing maintenance cost. Raised-bed farming — forming the cropping surface into elevated beds separated by furrows — is widely used on WA's waterlogging-prone duplex and heavier-textured soils, particularly in higher-rainfall southern areas, to keep the active root zone above the perched or seasonally high water table rather than attempting to remove the water entirely. Perennial pasture phases in rotation — deep-rooted perennial grasses, lucerne, or saltbush on more saline ground — directly address the recharge mechanism identified in the previous slide by reintroducing a vegetation type that uses water year-round, lowering the water table over a multi-year rotation rather than relying on engineering drainage alone.

Part 10: Evapotranspiration — Field to Satellite

Atmospheric draw, crop coefficients, and SEBAL energy balance modeling

Evapotranspiration (ET) Dissected

ET combines two distinct physical pathways into a single operational number:

Evaporation

Direct vapor loss from the soil surface and moisture caught on leaf exteriors.

Transpiration

Active plant draw through roots, exiting via leaf stomata to drive internal cooling.

These interconnected fluxes are measured together because they occur simultaneously within developing crop canopies.

Define evapotranspiration (ET) precisely as the combined water loss from a cropped or vegetated landscape via two simultaneous physical processes: evaporation (direct loss of water from the soil surface and from water intercepted on plant leaves) and transpiration (water drawn up through plant roots and lost as vapour through leaf stomata, the same physiological process already introduced as the basis of thermal crop-water-stress sensing in the companion Sensors chapter). Explain why these two processes are conventionally measured and reported as a single combined number rather than separately: in a growing crop canopy, the two fluxes occur simultaneously from intermingled surfaces and are extremely difficult to physically separate in the field, even though their relative contribution shifts predictably as a crop canopy develops from bare soil (evaporation-dominated) to full cover (transpiration-dominated).

The Crop Coefficient Framework

$$ET_c = ET_0 \times K_c$$

  • $ET_0$ (Reference ET): Weather-driven potential water loss from a standardized grass surface, calculated via the Penman-Monteith equation.
  • $K_c$ (Crop Coefficient): A dynamic correction scalar that tracks crop canopy maturity:
    • Emergence: Low $K_c$ (bare soil evaporation dominates)
    • Peak Canopy: Maximum $K_c$ (peak transpiration during vegetative growth)
    • Senescence: Falling $K_c$ (canopy dries down ahead of harvest)
Introduce the standard framework used to convert a generic atmospheric water-demand estimate into a crop- and stage-specific water-use figure. Reference evapotranspiration (ET0) represents the water loss expected from a standardised, well-watered reference surface under the prevailing weather conditions — temperature, humidity, wind, and solar radiation, measured by exactly the weather station instrumentation introduced in the companion Sensors chapter — and is conventionally estimated using the Penman-Monteith equation. Crop evapotranspiration (ETc) scales ET0 by a crop coefficient (Kc) that varies through the growing season as canopy cover and physiological activity change — low during early establishment when bare soil dominates the surface, peaking near full canopy cover and active growth, and declining again during senescence — directly connecting to the canopy development staging introduced in the companion Crop Spatial Variability chapter.

Driving Water Balance Decisions

  • Irrigation Tracking: $ET_c$ defines the exact water volume consumed, allowing growers to match applications to actual crop demand.
  • Dryland Yield Projections: Integrating regional $ET_c$ patterns against AWC profiles maps terminal drought risks in Mediterranean environments.
  • Recharge Regulation: Maximizing seasonal $ET$ via deep-rooted vegetation reduces water table recharge, checking dryland salinity expansion.
Connect ET directly to practical water management decisions. For irrigation scheduling, ETc represents the water a crop is actually using at a given growth stage, and replacing approximately that amount, adjusted for effective rainfall and the available water capacity developed in Part 5, is the foundation of demand-based irrigation scheduling on WA's irrigated horticultural and pasture systems. For dryland systems, the gap between seasonal rainfall and crop ET, integrated with the available water capacity and root zone access developed in Part 6, is the central determinant of terminal drought risk under WA's Mediterranean rainfall pattern. For groundwater recharge estimation, the difference between rainfall and ET is, to a first approximation, the water available to become recharge — directly connecting ET back to the dryland salinity mechanism developed in Part 9, since increasing ET through deep-rooted perennial vegetation is precisely the lever used to reduce recharge and manage rising water tables.

Satellite Energy Balances: SEBAL

SEBAL resolves the fundamental surface energy balance equation across wide areas using satellite imagery:

$$R_n = G + H + \lambda E$$

Where $R_n$ is net radiation, $G$ is soil heat flux, $H$ is sensible heat, and $\lambda E$ is latent heat (ET).

graph TD Rn[Net Radiation] --> G[Soil Heat Flux] Rn --> H[Sensible Heat: Warming Air] Rn --> LE[Latent Heat: Evapotranspiration] style LE fill:#27348B,color:#fff

The Calibration Step: Anchor pixels isolate a "cold" wet field (maximum ET) and a "hot" bare zone (zero ET) to calibrate sensible heat extraction across the image.

Introduce the Surface Energy Balance Algorithm for Land (SEBAL) as a method for estimating actual evapotranspiration across an entire landscape from satellite imagery, without needing a ground-based weather station at every location. SEBAL solves the surface energy balance equation — incoming net radiation is partitioned into sensible heat (warming the air), soil heat flux, and latent heat (the energy consumed by evapotranspiration) — using satellite thermal imagery to estimate surface temperature and satellite optical imagery to estimate surface reflectance and vegetation cover. The key calibration step: the algorithm identifies a "cold" reference pixel within the image, assumed to be well-watered and transpiring at close to the maximum rate ET0 would predict, and a "hot" reference pixel, assumed to be dry with negligible evapotranspiration, and uses the temperature contrast between them to calibrate the sensible heat flux estimate across every other pixel, with latent heat — and therefore actual ET — calculated as the remaining balance.

Remote ET in Field Practice

  • Spatial Advantage: Delivers continuous, full-field water consumption mapping rather than relying on interpolation between isolated weather stations.
  • Cloud Cover Limitations: Requires clear, cloud-free thermal overpasses to obtain accurate surface temperature data.
  • Calibration Vulnerabilities: Anchor assumptions can struggle if an image lacks a truly wet or completely dry reference point.
Discuss the practical value and limitations of SEBAL-style remote ET estimation for WA water management. Its principal strength is spatial completeness: rather than relying on a small number of point weather stations (Part 10's earlier slides) and assuming their readings apply uniformly across a landscape, SEBAL produces an actual ET estimate for every pixel in a satellite scene, capturing genuine spatial variability in water use driven by differences in crop type, vegetation cover, and water stress across a region — directly relevant to regional water accounting in irrigation districts and to monitoring the perennial-vegetation recharge management strategies introduced in Part 9 at landscape scale. Its limitations mirror those already raised for passive optical and thermal sensing in the companion Sensors chapter: accuracy depends on cloud-free thermal imagery, the cold- and hot-pixel calibration assumption can break down where no genuinely well-watered or genuinely dry reference surface exists within a given scene, and results still benefit from ground-truthing against point weather station and soil-water data wherever it is available.

Part 11: Conservation Practices & Synthesis

Erosion management and resolving the Narembeen template

Soil & Water Conservation Practices

  • Contour Farming: Aligning seeding paths along topographical contours to interrupt surface runoff velocities.
  • Graded Interception Banks: Catches moving surface runoff and routes fluids safely toward stable paddock outlets.
  • Terracing Systems: Highly effective for steep slopes globally, but rarely cost-effective on WA's vast, gentle broadacre contours.
  • Cover Cropping: Utilizing off-season crops to shield soil structure and limit erosion risks.
Survey the major soil and water conservation practices used to manage the spatial variability and degradation risks developed across this lecture. Contour farming and graded banks slow runoff and reduce water erosion (Part 5) by working with rather than across the slope on more undulating country. Terracing, common in steeper hillside agricultural landscapes elsewhere in the world, sees far more limited use in WA's generally gentler broadacre topography, where contour banks achieve a similar runoff-management function at lower capital cost. Cover cropping — maintaining a living or residue soil cover between cash crops to protect structure and reduce erosion — is the general international practice; introduce the WA-specific equivalent developed fully in the next slide.

WA's Conservation Framework

Mediterranean rainfall patterns and short seeding windows make traditional cover crops impractical for broadacre setups.

Instead, WA pioneered a distinct system centered on Stubble Retention & No-Till Seeding:

  • Previous season crop stubble is left standing or spread on the surface rather than burned.
  • Protects delicate topsoil from raindrop-driven surface sealing.
  • Provides vital armor against severe summer wind erosion on exposed sands.
  • Gradually builds organic matter to improve long-term structural stability.
Explain why WA broadacre cropping has developed a distinctive conservation practice rather than adopting cover cropping in the form common in higher-rainfall cropping regions elsewhere. Under WA's Mediterranean rainfall pattern and tight, narrow seeding windows, establishing and then terminating a dedicated cover crop ahead of the cash crop is rarely practical or affordable across the large areas typical of WA broadacre farms. Instead, stubble retention — leaving the previous season's crop residue standing or on the surface rather than burning or fully incorporating it, combined with no-till or minimum-till seeding directly into that residue — has become the dominant WA conservation practice, delivering many of the same benefits as cover cropping: protecting the soil surface from raindrop impact and the structural sealing introduced in Part 5, reducing wind erosion risk on exposed sandy soils, and contributing organic matter that supports the structural stability developed in Part 3 — all without requiring a separate crop establishment and termination cycle.
